And so it begins

2007-02-03 to 2007-02-04

Well, I find myself in Spain, at a computer in a very clean hostel with a hangover. How best to piece together what happened yesterday. It´s safe to say that 4 am is a time created by an old testament God and is a time neither I nor Duane ever wish to frequent again.

So Barcelona. A remarkably beautiful city, but i fear Gaudi was a touch mad. They call it art and it certainly draws the eye. The Sagrada Familia is a beautiful church, but after all this time i really feel they should have got round to finishing it. Just lazy if you ask me.

Spent the rest of the day wondering aound parks, going to see the sea and spending money climbing just about any momument we could. Bad news for the budget, but we have decided to fast for a while so no doubt we will make it up.

In the evening we walked for miles to go to the Font Magica just off Plaça d´Espanya to watch the coloured fountains while listening to classical music. I´m begining to feel very cultured and if anyone comes to Barcelona it would be up there in my list of things to do.

Several bottles of wine later and some dancing in the street, we managed to negotiate the metro system home. I´m sure it didn´t need to be as hard as in was, but I blame the language barrier.

Apologies for spelling i`m a little drunk, Duane.

Having, however, discovered one or two gems amoungst the throng of discerning American art lovers battling for a glimspe of da Vinci`s "anti-climax in blue and green," we realised that the day was spent and our bed did beckon.

RIght kids, last day. thanks for sticking with me. What was left ... ah yes the tower. Up and down was made quite tricky by the wind but with determination we scalled and at the top i won Eiffel tower top trumps. success.

Having spied the chuch containing the mortal remains of Napolean from the top we thought it our British duty to make sure the Corsican was not up to any of his trade mark mischief. As we walked down the champs de Mars, however, the skies behind the Eiffel tower grew very dark. As we turned into the church yard the hevans opened and a terrifying torent was unleashed upon us. By the time we made to ascend the steps of the church hail stones the size of musket balls were frustrating our advance and determination. That no Englishman should disturb the slumber of Napolean was surely the message sent from the skies and so we ducked down an alyway for a coffee. Within seconds of our tack once more the sun appeared to smile upon our stay with the French. SO with climatic civility restored and the Entante Cordiale intact we skipped back through the puddles of the churchyard wistleing `Rule Britannia` and leaving Bonaparte well alone.

We finished our stay in Paris the way it began. On the slopes of Monmartre drinking wine and singing.

The gateway to India

2007-03-06 to 2007-03-09

After a cramped long flight, all you really want is a cold shower and a chance to catch up on some lost sleep. Welcome to Mumbai. It`s the middle of the day (only 40 degrees) and you have no idea where you are or how to get to your hostel. Unfortuantely that also seems true for the taxi drivers.

The roads have few street names and the drivers are all manically insane, but with the help of a few locals we manage to find our hostel and prepare ourselves for our Mumbai experience.

It doesn`t seem too busy, but then we discover that we are infact 30 minutes by train from the tourist centre. A sense of dread appeared as we imagined what an Indian train might be like, but it was fairly empty with ceiling fans (standing room only) but a very efficient means of transport.

To cross the raod in Mumbai is to take your life into your own hands, but at any crossing point eventually the numbers become large enough for the cars to have to stop or cause a massacre. The sights of Mumbai are not numerous and the food and drink is "expensive" but we had some fun experiences. Being white gets you stared at in Mumbai, being outlandishly tall gets people not looking away when you catch their eye and walking alongside you for miles just to get a comparison. They are all friendly about it and you can have some entertaining conversations in broken English with them as you walk.

The rickshaw. A most noble means of transportation and several were used in our hunt for the consulate of Vietnam. There isn`t one (and if there is i don`t want to hear about it. I enjoy my ignorance), but at least we managed to accost a hospital porter to aid us in our search. he didn`t even want a tip.......most unusual!!!

To avid the general pandemonium of the city we also took some time to explore the wonders of elephante island (the British removed the statue of the elephant that gave the island its name, but strangely didn`t put it in the British museum!?!). Lots of caves, monkeys and cows. It was nice to be able to sit somewhere and not have someone try to sell you a giant inflatable balloon or magnets.

The train home showed Indian trains to be exactly what you would expect. The war cries as you entered a station from people about to board, the pushing, holding on for dear life and then off goes the train again. I felt like a battery farmed chicken, but got to talk to lots of Indians about cricket (they rarely talk about anything else).

Mumbai.....we probably spent too long there given what it had to offer, but certainly an experince i will remember and even, most of the time, enjoyed.
